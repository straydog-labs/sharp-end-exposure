The Research
Ten studies across four themes. Every design decision maps directly to peer-reviewed science.
How your zone is calculated
Confirm Zone asks 4 questions. Each answer adds points to a score. The score determines your zone. The weights come from research — not guesswork.
Q1
Breathing
Primary signal · Hanton et al. 2004
easy=0 · faster=1
shallow=2 · held=4
Q2
Gut sensation
Pre-conscious marker · Damasio 1994
settled=0 · flutter=1
tension=2
Q3
Crux response
Strongest indicator · Beilock & Carr 2001
none=0 · paused=1
backed off=2 · panicked=4
Q4
Body state
Overall activation · Van der Kolk 2014
loose=0 · activated=1
tight=2 · frozen=3
Comfort 0–4 · Learning 5–9 · Panic 10–12 · max score 12
In plain terms: these weights are an informed rule of thumb based on real research — not a clinically validated formula. The science tells us which signals matter most. The specific point values are our best current design, being refined as real data comes in.
Somatic signals
The body is already reporting
Breath and gut register threat before conscious awareness — the same signals tracked in Go Deeper — and the foundation of what the check-in measures.
›
2017Lisa Feldman Barrett — How Emotions Are Made
Emotions are not hardwired reactions — they are constructed from interoceptive signals, past experience, and cultural context. The same physiological activation can be experienced as excitement or terror depending on how the brain interprets it.
Why the tool never labels somatic signals as "fear." Restricted breathing is described as restricted breathing — not as danger.
ScoringHow this affects your zone result
Breath is weighted as the primary signal in Confirm Zone — the fastest-onset somatic marker (Hanton et al. 2004). Held breath scores highest (4 points). Gut sensation scores as secondary (max 2 points). Together they account for over half your zone calculation.
When breath and gut both signal high activation, the zone result will reflect that — even if the climb outcome (sent/fell) suggests otherwise. That divergence is the most useful data point in the app.
1994Antonio Damasio — Descartes' Error
Gut sensation functions as a pre-conscious decision marker. Patients with damaged somatic signal pathways made systematically worse decisions — even when they could explain the correct choice.
The pause screen creates conditions where gut markers can register before the second check-in. Gut shift is the primary pre-decision data point.
2015Sarah Garfinkel & Hugo Critchley — Biological Psychology
Interoceptive accuracy varies across individuals, including along neurodivergent lines, and is measurable and trainable over time.
Why "not paying attention to my breath" is a valid response — not a failure. The tool trains the noticing, not the result.
Arousal & performance
There is an optimal activation window
The same curve behind the three zones — Comfort, Learning, Panic. You see this every time you get a result.
›
1908Yerkes & Dodson — Journal of Comparative Neurology
Performance follows an inverted-U curve relative to arousal. The optimal window varies by task complexity — fine motor and judgment tasks require lower arousal.
Zone placement is a readout of where you sit on the arousal axis relative to this route today — not a capability ranking.
ScoringHow this affects your zone result
The zone thresholds (Comfort 0–4, Learning 5–9, Panic 10–12) are set to reflect the inverted-U curve: a wide Learning zone at the optimal activation peak, with Comfort and Panic as the tails. Crux response is weighted second-most-heavily (max 3 points) — breath carries the highest weight (max 4 points) — consistent with Beilock & Carr's finding that breakdown at the hardest moment is the clearest over-activation indicator.
Your score is not a grade. Comfort zone on a hard route and Comfort zone on an easy route mean different things — the arc over time is what reveals the pattern, not any single result. In plain terms: the scoring is an informed rule of thumb, not a clinical test. It's designed to reflect what the research says matters most.
2001Sian Beilock & Thomas Carr — Journal of Experimental Psychology
When pressure activates conscious monitoring of automated movement, expert performance breaks down across sports and genders.
Why "distracted" and "reactive" are the panic-zone failure modes.
→ In Confirm Zone: crux response's weighted contribution maxes at 3 points (breath carries the highest weight, at 4). "Panicked but recovered" scores 4. "Backed off" scores 2. "No standout moment" scores 0. The crux moment is where over-activation is most clearly expressed.
1997Claude Steele — American Psychologist, Stanford
Identity threat raises activation and impairs performance independently of actual capability.
Why zone placement carries no value judgment and language throughout is deliberately neutral.
Deliberate practice
What you notice determines what you keep
The session arc in Sessions visualises this — volume alone does not predict growth.
›
1993Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer — Psychological Review
Expert performance requires deliberate practice: structured repetition with specific process variables identified in advance and immediate feedback. Passive repetition produces plateaus, not expertise.
The process intention question is the direct application. Comfort zone guidance notes passive repetition produces limited transfer.
2006Carol Dweck — Mindset
A fixed-capability belief produces worse outcomes than a growth belief. Fixed mindset increases performance anxiety.
Why zone placement is never framed as a capability measure.
2003Arno Ilgner — The Rock Warrior's Way
Conscious risk-taking is a learnable skill. The decision not to attempt is as informative as the decision to proceed. Practitioner framework, not a controlled study.
Did Not Attempt is a first-class option. Motivation is assessed because conscious vs. reactive motivation produces different learning outcomes.
Body scanning
The body holds what the mind hasn't noticed yet
The same body schematic from the Prepare flow — backed by 30 years of MBSR research.
›
1990Jon Kabat-Zinn — Full Catastrophe Living
Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction centres the body scan as a core practice. Hundreds of RCTs document measurable increases in body awareness and reductions in stress-related holding.
The body scan follows the MBSR sequence. Nothing to report is equally valid — it records a clean baseline.
1997Peter Levine — Waking the Tiger
Unresolved activation is held in specific tissue regions as chronic muscular tension below the conscious awareness threshold.
Why tension before a trad lead reads differently from tension after a gym session.
2014Bessel van der Kolk — The Body Keeps the Score
Repeated overwhelming experiences without conscious processing can condition the nervous system toward threat response rather than adaptation. The body encodes these patterns below conscious awareness.
Why the tool distinguishes conscious Panic-zone entry from reactive Panic — and why the process prompt asks "is this a conscious decision?" not "are you scared?"
ScoringHow this affects your zone result
Body state (Q4 in Confirm Zone) scores 0–3: loose/fluid = 0, activated = 1, tight/gripped = 2, shut down/frozen = 3. "Shut down or frozen" is the highest body-state signal — consistent with Van der Kolk's description of a nervous system in overwhelm rather than engagement.
A climber who feels tight but engaged (score 2) reads differently from one who feels frozen (score 3). The distinction matters and the scoring reflects it. "Didn't notice" scores 1 — neutral, not zero — following Garfinkel & Critchley's finding that low interoceptive awareness is itself a data point.
2018Cynthia Price & Carol Hooven — Frontiers in Psychology
Interoceptive awareness skills are teachable and directly improve emotion regulation and decision-making.
Why the body scan asks two follow-up questions: what are you noticing, and is this typical? Characterising sensation is where the skill develops.
Sharp End Exposure collects self-reported data. Somatic signals vary significantly between people — neurodivergent climbers may experience these questions differently, and that's valid data too. Not a clinical assessment. All decisions remain with the climber.
Temporal distance & the check-in
Checking in days before a climb is scientifically valid
Psychological distance creates a window for clearer somatic reading than immediate pre-climb activation.
›
2010Trope & Liberman — Construal Level Theory · Psychological Review
Psychological distance — including temporal distance — changes how threats are construed. Events further away in time are processed more abstractly and with less reactive activation, creating a window for deliberate assessment that closes as the event approaches.
Why the check-in works days or weeks before a climb — not only at the base. Temporal distance enables clearer somatic reading. Checking in at the wall captures peak activation; checking in days before captures the full decision window.
2004Hanton, Thomas & Maynard — Journal of Sport Sciences
Somatic anxiety frequencies begin increasing seven days before competition — not just in the final hour. The body starts signalling the challenge well before the event is imminent.
The check-in captures a signal that is already present days before a climb. Checking in early is not premature — it catches the full decision window before peak activation narrows perception.
2001Woodman & Hardy — Journal of Sports Sciences (meta-analysis)
Across a large meta-analysis, both cognitive and somatic anxiety showed meaningful patterns across the full pre-event window — not just minutes before. The relationship between anxiety and performance is measurable days out.
The decision window is wider than it feels. A check-in two days before a route is capturing real signal — not speculation.
Breathwork & autonomic regulation
Your breath is the fastest lever you have
Voluntary breathing changes your physiological state in under 60 seconds — the research basis for the breathwork step in Prepare for a climb.
›
2018Zaccaro et al. — Frontiers in Human Neuroscience
A systematic review of slow breathing techniques found consistent activation of the parasympathetic nervous system, reductions in cortisol, and improvements in emotional regulation. Effects emerged within a single session of 5-6 breath cycles per minute.
Why the Prepare for a climb flow starts with breath — not questions. Slowing the breath first creates a cleaner baseline for the somatic check-in that follows.
2015Jerath et al. — Medical Hypotheses
Long slow breathing at 5-6 breaths per minute optimizes heart rate variability and shifts autonomic balance from sympathetic toward parasympathetic dominance. The effect is measurable within 90 seconds.
The 4-count in, 4-count hold, 6-count out pattern in Prepare for a climb is derived from this research. The longer exhale specifically activates the vagus nerve.
Visualization & motor imagery
Your nervous system doesn't distinguish imagination from reality
Mental rehearsal activates the same neural pathways as physical climbing — the research basis for the visualization step in Prepare for a climb.
›
1994Driskell, Copper & Moran — Journal of Applied Psychology
A meta-analysis of 35 studies found mental practice significantly improves motor performance, with the largest effects on tasks with high cognitive demand. The effect size is comparable to a reduced physical practice session.
Route visualization is not superstition — it is neurologically equivalent to a practice attempt. The somatic response during visualization is real physiological data worth logging.
2005Guillot & Collet — Journal of Sports Sciences
Neuroimaging studies show motor imagery activates the same cortical and subcortical networks as physical movement execution, including primary motor cortex, premotor cortex, and cerebellum.
What you notice somatically during visualization — grip tightening, breathing changing, gut responding — is your nervous system running a real simulation of the route.
2001Holmes & Collins — The Sport Psychologist
The PETTLEP model identifies seven components that maximize effectiveness: Physical, Environment, Task, Timing, Learning, Emotion, Perspective. Imagery matching actual execution conditions produces the strongest transfer.
The visualization prompt in Prepare for a climb is structured around PETTLEP — imagining yourself on the specific route, in real time, with your actual emotional state present.
