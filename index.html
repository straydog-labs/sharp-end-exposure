How it works
Assessment flow
Exposure map — three activation zones
Comfort
Perceived demand within current experience range.
Learning
Activation within optimal adaptation range.
Panic
Perceived demand outpacing current experience.
Sharp End Exposure is a pre-climb psychological readiness tool. It records self-reported data across seven baseline questions, maps placement across three activation zones, and tracks patterns over time through climb and fall logging.

All data is self-reported. The tool does not assess clinical readiness or make recommendations. Decisions about what to attempt remain with the climber.
01
Assess your readiness
7 self-reported questions covering climbing style, terrain experience, prior attempts, somatic state (breathing), intention, mental state, and a second somatic check. Responses are scored and mapped to one of three activation zones: comfort, learning, or panic. Zone placement reflects reported readiness — not performance capability.
02
Route analysis on the route
10 route-specific questions covering bolt spacing, crux fall zone, protection visibility, swing risk, physical condition, partner dynamic, session position, and fall consequence exposure. Responses refine your Exposure Map placement based on the specific demands of the route.
03
Log this climb
After each climb, log outcome data: fall type, terrain, grade, somatic state during the climb, body position, mental state at crux, and approach. This data populates the terrain radar and exposure map, building a visual record of where experience has been gained and where gaps remain.
04
Track your patterns
Session history displays zone distribution, exposure map dot placement, zone progression timeline, and terrain radar. Single sessions provide a snapshot. Longitudinal data across multiple sessions surfaces activation patterns, terrain gaps, and readiness trends over time.
Answer based on current state, not aspirational state. The tool surfaces accurate data only when responses reflect actual readiness — not desired readiness. Zone placement carries no value judgment. Comfort, learning, and panic zones reflect activation levels, not performance quality. Use the data to inform decisions. The decision itself remains yours.

The Research
The psychological and physiological research underpinning Sharp End Exposure. Each section references peer-reviewed research on activation, experiential learning, and somatic awareness as they relate to performance and risk decision-making. Individual nervous system responses vary — these frameworks represent population-level findings, not individual prescriptions.
Optimal Activation
The learning window
Research suggests there is an optimal level of mental activation for skill acquisition — enough arousal to focus attention, but not so much that it interferes with performance. For many climbers, this window is where the most meaningful learning tends to happen. Too little activation and attention drifts. Too much and perception narrows. Individual responses vary — notice where your window tends to be.

Yerkes, R.M. & Dodson, J.D. (1908). The relation of strength of stimulus to rapidity of habit-formation. Journal of Comparative Neurology and Psychology.
Somatic Awareness
Your body as a data source
Interoception — the ability to notice signals from inside your body — is a trainable skill. Research suggests climbers who develop greater body awareness tend to make more accurate assessments of their readiness and fatigue. Breathing is one of the most accessible interoceptive anchors — it changes with activation in ways that are often noticeable before conscious awareness catches up. Notice what your breath does at the base of the route.

Craig, A.D. (2002). How do you feel? Interoception: the sense of the physiological condition of the body. Nature Reviews Neuroscience.
Individual Nervous System Variability
One size does not fit all
Autonomic nervous system responses to stress are not uniform across people. Research shows significant individual variability in how sympathetic and parasympathetic activation manifests — what triggers a fear response, how it presents in the body, and what helps regulate it. This is especially relevant for neurodivergent individuals, whose stress responses may not follow typical patterns. The zones in this tool reflect broad tendencies in research — your experience may differ. That difference is not a problem. It is information.

Porges, S.W. (2011). The Polyvagal Theory. Norton. / Individual differences in autonomic nervous system reactivity.
Fall Psychology
Fear is learned — and it can change
Fear of falling involves both innate and conditioned components. Research indicates that conditioned fear responses — shaped by prior experience and perceived consequence — are modifiable through systematic, progressive exposure. Controlled exposure to falling in lower-consequence terrain is associated with reduced fear response over time in many individuals. Session data tracked by this tool — activation zone, terrain, fall response, return to climbing — provides longitudinal indicators of whether that relationship is shifting.

Rachman, S. (1990). Fear and Courage. W.H. Freeman. / Bandura, A. (1977). Self-efficacy: Toward a unifying theory of behavioral change. Psychological Review.
Stress and Performance
High activation as a readiness signal
Under high perceived stress, research suggests that for many people, judgment and fine motor control can be affected. This tends to be a physiological response to perceived threat — not a character issue, not a capability issue. Recognizing high activation as a readiness signal rather than a character judgment tends to produce better decisions about when and how to engage with challenging terrain. Notice the signal. Then decide.

Lazarus, R.S. (1991). Emotion and Adaptation. Oxford University Press. / Hanin, Y.L. (2000). Emotions in Sport. Human Kinetics.
Deliberate Practice
Repetition is not enough
Research on skill acquisition indicates that practice volume is a weaker predictor of adaptation than attentional quality during practice. Repetition without directed focus tends to reinforce existing movement patterns rather than produce new ones. Comfort zone climbing with deliberate attentional focus produces measurable skill development. Without directed attention, adaptation is limited.

Ericsson, K.A., Krampe, R.T., & Tesch-Romer, C. (1993). The role of deliberate practice in the acquisition of expert performance. Psychological Review.
Process vs Outcome
What you control tends to matter more
Performance psychology research consistently points to process focus — attention on what you can control — as more predictive of performance than outcome focus. Sharp End Exposure asks about intention, mental state, breathing, and fall response rather than grade, because that is where the data that reflects growth tends to live.

Dweck, C.S. (2006). Mindset: The New Psychology of Success. Random House.
Sharp End Exposure is a self-report data collection tool, not a clinical instrument. The frameworks referenced draw on established sport psychology, exercise science, and performance research. All outputs are derived from self-reported responses and should be interpreted as pattern data — not definitive individual assessment. Nervous system responses vary significantly across individuals. Divergence from population-level research findings is expected and does not indicate error in the data.
