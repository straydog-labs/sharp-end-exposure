How it works
01
Assess your readiness
7 questions before you climb. Based on your responses, Sharp End Exposure places you on the Exposure Map — in your comfort zone, learning zone, or panic zone. Each zone has a different implication for how you approach the climb.
02
Go deeper on the route
10 additional questions about the specific route — bolt distance, crux fall zone, physical state, partner dynamic, session context. This refines your Exposure Map placement based on the actual demands of the climb, not just your general readiness.
03
Log your falls
After you climb, log what happened. Fall type, wall angle, stress response, whether you got back on. Over time this builds your terrain radar — a visual map of where you have actual fall experience and where the gaps are.
04
Track your patterns
Your session history shows where you have been landing over time — comfort, learning, or panic — and how your terrain experience is building. Single sessions give you a snapshot. Multiple sessions reveal your patterns.
A note on how to use it
Answer honestly, not aspirationally. The tool only works if your responses reflect where you actually are — not where you want to be. A comfort zone result is not failure. A panic zone result is not weakness. They are data. The goal is to use that data to make more deliberate decisions about the risk you are taking.

The Research
The psychology behind Sharp End Exposure — why the zones exist, what the questions are measuring, and what the data suggests about how climbers develop.
Optimal Activation
The learning window
Performance research consistently shows there is an optimal level of mental activation for any given task. Too little — and attention drifts, effort drops, learning slows. Too much — and judgment narrows, fine motor control can degrade, risk assessment becomes less reliable. The zone in between is where skill acquisition tends to peak. Sharp End Exposure calls this the learning zone. It is not where you feel most comfortable. Research suggests it is where you improve most.
Fall Psychology
Fear is learned — and it can change
Fear of falling is not fixed. Research suggests it is largely a learned response — shaped by past experience, perceived consequence, and the stories we tell ourselves about what falling means. Controlled, deliberate exposure to falling in progressively challenging terrain tends to reduce that fear response over time. That is what the fall log is tracking — not just what happened, but how your relationship with falling changes across sessions.
Stress and Performance
High activation is a readiness signal, not a character judgment
Under high perceived stress, the brain tends to prioritize threat detection over performance. Research suggests this can affect judgment, working memory, and fine motor control — not because something is wrong with the climber, but because that is how threat response tends to work. Recognizing high activation as a readiness signal — rather than a character flaw — tends to produce better decisions about when and how to engage with challenging terrain.
Deliberate Practice
Repetition is not enough
Research on skill acquisition suggests volume of practice matters less than the quality of attention brought to it. Repetition without a specific focus reinforces existing patterns rather than building new ones. Deliberate practice — defined by a specific intention, attention to process, and reflection on what happened — produces skill development even on familiar terrain. Comfort zone climbing with a deliberate focus is not wasted climbing. Without one, it often is.
Process vs Outcome
What you control matters more than what you want
Performance psychology research consistently shows that focusing on what you can control — preparation, attention, response to difficulty — produces better outcomes than focusing on the result. Outcome focus narrows attention to success or failure. Process focus expands it to the specific actions that lead to performance. Sharp End Exposure asks about intention, mental state, and fall response rather than grade — because that is where the data that predicts growth tends to live.
Sharp End Exposure is a self-assessment tool, not a clinical instrument. The frameworks referenced here draw on established sport psychology and performance research. Results are based on self-reported responses and should be interpreted as one data point among many — not as a definitive assessment of any individual.
